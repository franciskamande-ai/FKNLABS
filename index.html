Research Publications

The Skyscraper Effect — Impact on Financial Markets

The Skyscraper Effect hypothesizes that record-breaking infrastructure projects, particularly skyscrapers, can act as economic and psychological signals affecting market behavior.

Key Findings & Examples:

  • Historical correlation: Cities with record-breaking skyscrapers often experience short-term stock market volatility reflecting investor sentiment and capital allocation.
  • Psychological impact: Iconic buildings can influence investor confidence and local economic optimism.
  • Case Study — Burj Khalifa (Dubai): The construction coincided with increased speculative investment inflows into local real estate and stock markets.
  • Case Study — Shanghai Tower: Market data indicated increased domestic and foreign investment interest post-completion.

This research highlights how physical mega-projects can signal economic strength, influencing trading strategies and macroeconomic models. Future work involves integrating these signals into predictive ML models for market behavior.

The Quantified Anatomy of Retail Trading Failure

This research deconstructs the 95% retail trader failure rate through a multi-layered mathematical framework, demonstrating it's not an anomaly but an emergent property of the trading system itself.

Key Mathematical Frameworks:

  • The Cost Drag Theorem: Proof that transaction costs transform fair markets into guaranteed losing systems
  • Trading Ruin Theorem: Adaptation of Gambler's Ruin theory to leveraged financial trading
  • Behavioral Degradation Model: How Prospect Theory systematically degrades win rates and payoff ratios
  • Leverage Acceleration Effect: Mathematical demonstration of how leverage non-linearly increases ruin probability

This research provides a complete mathematical explanation for retail trading failure rates, combining transaction cost analysis, behavioral finance, and risk theory. The framework can be used to develop more robust trading systems and risk management protocols.
